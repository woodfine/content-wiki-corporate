MEMORANDUM
Jurisdictional Fracture /  Fractura Jurisdiccional
There seems to be a fair amount Jurisdictional Fracture in the terminology we cover.
The term Public, Non-Listed is a valid and common status in Canada and the US (e.g., Reporting Issuer or Public Non-Traded REIT).
However, in Spain (SOCIMI) and Mexico (FIBRA), the tax-exempt status is legally tied to being Listed (Cotizada).
The Trap: If you tell a Spanish investor you are a Public Non-Listed SOCIMI, they will say that is impossible.
To get the 0% tax rate, a SOCIMI must be listed on an exchange (BME Growth or Main Market).
The Ambiguity:
In English (CA/US): Listed means Trading on the NYSE/TSX. You can be Public without being Listed.
In Spanish (ES/MX): Cotizada (Listed) is the definition of being Public.
There is no widely used category for Public but not Listed equity.
The Bridge Term: Public Non-Traded
To speak English that translates cleanly into Spanish without triggering legal confusion, you must stop using Public, Non-Listed as a global term.
Instead, focus on the Regulatory Status (the safety) and the Liquidity Mechanism (the transferability).
Trap A: Reporting Issuer
The Problem: Reporting Issuer is a specific term under the Canadian Securities Act. It has no direct translation.
Spanish Risk: Translating it as Emisor de Informes sounds like a newsletter publisher.
Mexico Risk: They use Emisora (Issuer) but imply Emisora Bursátil (Stock Market Issuer).
The Bridge Term: Regulated Issuer or Regulated Reporting Entity.
Why: Regulated (Regulada) implies you file papers with the government (CNMV/CNBV/SEC/BCSC). It is true in all four countries.
Trap B: Listed vs. Traded
The Problem:
Canada/US: You can be Listed (on the board) but not Traded (no volume). Or Public but Unlisted.
Spain/Mexico: Cotizada implies both. If you are on the exchange, you are Cotizada.
The Bridge Term: Exchange-Qualified.
Why: It says you meet the standards of the exchange (for your debt or equity) without forcing the Non-Listed label that breaks the Spanish logic.
Trap C: Registered
The Problem:
English: Registered means you filed with the SEC/Commission.
Spanish: Registrada can mean just Incorporated (in the Mercantile Registry). The higher standard is Inscrita (Inscribed in the National Securities Registry).
The Bridge Term: Securities-Registered.

Business of the Trust /  Negocio del Fideicomiso

Legal Structure: Fideicomiso de Inversión en Bienes Raíces FIBRA (Real Estate Investment Trust).
Governing Laws: Ley del Impuesto sobre la Renta (LISR, Art. 187 & 188), Ley del Mercado de Valores (LMV), and Resolución Miscelánea Fiscal (Rule 3.21.3.2 for Private FIBRAs).
This structure allows for 100% real estate development (build-to-lease) while maintaining tax transparency (flow-through), offering freely transferable equity (via Indeval), and issuing publicly traded debt.

Tax Transparency (Flow-Through Status) /  Transparencia Fiscal (Estado de Transparencia Fiscal)
Under Article 188 of the LISR, the FIBRA is fiscally transparent.
0% Corporate Tax: The Trust pays no income tax on rental income or capital gains.
The Development Rule: To maintain 0% tax, properties built by the FIBRA must be held for leasing for at least 4 years after completion.
Compliance Rule: At least 70% of the Trust's assets must be invested in real estate destined for lease.

Freely Transferable Equity (CBFIs) /  Capital De Libre Transferencia ( CBFIs )
To create liquidity for investors while maintaining Private control, the Trust registers as a Private FIBRA.
The Instrument: It issues CBFIs (Certificados Bursátiles Fiduciarios Inmobiliarios).
The Registry: Certificates are registered in the National Securities Registry (RNV) and deposited in Indeval.
Restricted Regime: To avoid market volatility, CBFIs are not listed on an open board but are transferable via Broker-to-Broker settlement.

Secondary Market: Who Qualifies & Transfer Protocol / Mercado Secundario: Quién Califica y Protocolo de Transferencia
Resident & Qualified Restrictions / Restricciones para Residentes y Calificados
Residents Only: Restricted to Tax Residents of Mexico. This eliminates the need for international tax treaty navigation and simplifies FATCA/CRS compliance.
Qualified Investors: Restricted to Institutional or Qualified Investors (Hedge funds, Pension funds, or High-Net-Worth individuals meeting CNBV wealth thresholds).

Self-Executing Transfer Protocol  / Protocolo De Transferencia De Ejecución Automática
The Certificate: Transfers are initiated by a Transfer of Ownership Certificate signed by both Buyer and Seller.
Affidavit Model: By signing, parties attest the Buyer meets the Residency and Qualified Investor criteria.
No Consent Required: No formal vote or discretionary approval from the Technical Committee or Trustee is required.
The signed Transfer of Ownership Certificate acts as the Instruction for settlement.
Mandated Processing: The Trustee Bank is mandated to process the Indeval settlement immediately upon receipt of the completed form.
